About this blog

Reading every gamebook
I can get my hands on

When I was a kid, I read a handful of Fighting Fantasy books and loved them — the dice, the choices, the feeling that the story was actually mine. Then they disappeared from my life. My mother thought they weren't proper reading. I moved on to other things.

Years later, older and more stubborn, I decided to go back and read all of them properly. Not just the ones I remembered — all of them. Fighting Fantasy, Lone Wolf, Sorcery!, Choose Your Own Adventure, the obscure ones, the modern ones, the Twine stuff that lives online. This blog is the record of that.

The gamebook is, at its best, a remarkable thing — a novel that asks something of you. It requires participation. You have to show up.

I'm not an academic and I'm not approaching this as a scholar. I'm a reader who finds this format genuinely compelling and wants to write about it honestly — what works, what doesn't, what holds up after forty years and what doesn't. I'll tell you if I cheated. I'll tell you if the book is unfair. I'll tell you if I loved it anyway.

What I cover

The plan is to work through as much of the catalogue as I can, roughly in chronological order within each series, with essays and features in between when something is worth saying that doesn't fit a review.

Fighting Fantasy
The series that started it all. Over 60 books spanning 1982 to present.
Lone Wolf
Joe Dever's 28-book epic — arguably the most ambitious gamebook series ever made.
Sorcery!
Steve Jackson's four-part masterwork, with one of the genre's best magic systems.
Choose Your Own Adventure
The American originals. More accessible, occasionally stranger than you remember.
Twine & Modern IF
The genre didn't die — it went online. Some of the best work is being made right now.
Essays & Features
Anything worth saying that doesn't fit neatly into a review format.

A note on honesty

I'm not going to pretend these books are all good. Some of them are extraordinary. Some are mediocre. Some are actively unfair in ways that would be unacceptable today. I'll say all of that, because the point isn't nostalgia — it's the honest experience of reading.

I'll also tell you when I cheated. I cheat. Anyone who claims they've never put their finger in the page is either lying or has a very different relationship with these books than I do.

No star ratings. Just words. If you want a number out of ten, this probably isn't the blog for you.
