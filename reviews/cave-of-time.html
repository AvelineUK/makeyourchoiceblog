The Cave of Time

Short, sharp, and with plenty of existential dread.

The Cave of Time was first published in 1979, written by Edward Packard, and is the first Choose Your Own Adventure book and the book that launched Chooseco publishing empire.

The synopsis is about as simple as they come. You're a young person who, while visiting their uncle, comes upon a cave that's recently been revealed to the world. You enter it, and then everything goes completely off the rails. That cave, of course, is the eponymous Cave of Time.

When the book arrived, I have to admit I was surprised by how small it was. Not only does it have under 150 pages, but those pages are small and this book will comfortably fit into a large pocket. Which is why I was puzzled when the cover proudly announced "40 endings." I was curious to know how this book could possibly fit in so much variety.

Well, I soon had my answer. Without meaning to spoil anything, the book works something like this: you choose to go left or right, and will eventually pick an opening to walk through. This opening takes you to some point in history, either past or present. Then you either die immediately, die after some period of exploration, end up residing in that time period, or re-enter the cave to try to find your way home.

Paths are short, and there was, at no stage, any prior warning that any particular path might be a bad choice. And some of the outcomes are genuinely bleak. During my very first adventure, I found myself on Earth around the time the Sun was reaching the end of its life: the planet was scorching hot, and I died. Just like that. Later, I found myself in Medieval Scotland, where I was eaten by the Loch Ness monster. In another, I was bombed during the Blitz. I had to double-check the target age range. Nine to twelve years old, apparently. I'm not entirely sure that instilling a deep existential dread in children is the best lesson a book can teach, but I will say this: the sheer absurdity of some of the outcomes made me laugh. Dear reader, I ended up on the Titanic, and even with my knowledge of what was going to happen, my character was completely powerless to stop the tragedy from unfolding.

This might sound like criticism, and it both is and isn't. The Cave of Time was the first of a brand new genre when it was published, and it was written to be accessible to children, so expecting complexity or mechanical depth simply isn't fair. But giving it a pass for its shortcomings doesn't mean those shortcomings aren't real. "Choose Your Own Adventure" is a misnomer; a better description might be "Hope for the Best on What Is Almost Certainly a Very Short, Fatal Journey." Less catchy, I know, but more accurate. The one saving grace is that each adventure is short enough that you don't feel you've wasted much time when it abruptly ends. It's difficult to feel annoyed about wasting five or ten minutes.

The Cave of Time has a surreal atmosphere. Not only does the book seem to draw inspiration from Alice in Wonderland, but because the book aims for width rather than depth, there's never time to really engage with anyone you meet. Friendships are created and ended within paragraphs, and you have very little control over them. In my opinion, depth is always better than width. After all, why bother having forty possible endings if I don't feel connected to any of them?

One aspect of the book genuinely surprised me: the sophistication of the writing. It never talks down to its target audience, nine to twelve year olds, at any stage. The problem is that any child clever enough to read it with ease will almost certainly not feel satisfied by the adventures they've chosen. The writing is better than the format deserves.

Verdict

I'm struggling to end this review with a pithy summary, and I think that's appropriate. The Cave of Time resists easy categorisation. Whether it's even a gamebook is itself an interesting question: there are no dice, no statistics, no loot. It's just you and your choices. Despite everything, I would encourage people to seek it out at their local library and give it a read. If it strikes a chord, there are many more CYOA books out there to explore, and I do plan to cover as many of them as I can. But I couldn't recommend buying it blind. It is entertaining, just not in the way I was hoping.
