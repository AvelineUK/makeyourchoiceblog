The Warlock of Firetop Mountain

Over forty years old, unfair, but still worth every minute.

The Warlock of Firetop Mountain was first published in 1982 and was written by Steve Jackson and Ian Livingstone. It is the first Fighting Fantasy book, and one of the first adventure gamebooks to gain widespread popularity.

Although this is my second review, The Warlock of Firetop Mountain was the first gamebook I read after rediscovering this childhood hobby as an adult. As with all my reviews, I decided to abide by the rules and play the book as it was intended. I played through it twice. Both times I rolled 10 for skill (more than enough, as it turned out) and my stamina and luck were similarly comfortable. On my first run I was genuinely worried that an 8 for luck would cause me problems, but it never did.

And so, after more than twenty-five years, I was playing a gamebook again. I know I was overflowing with nostalgia, but there's something truly magical about the format. Part puzzle, part self-directed adventure, part escapism. I was so pleased when the feeling I had as a kid returned to me almost immediately.

The Warlock of Firetop Mountain is, simply put, a dungeon. A network of passages with rooms branching off them, and three primary paths you can take. Because this is a spoiler-free review, I won't say which path is the correct one, but there is a correct one, and on my first run I chose about as wrongly as it's possible to choose. The Warlock of Firetop Mountain is cruel: you won't find out until you're nearly at the end.

Which brings me to my main criticism. There is no indication at the start of the book what you need to do to succeed. You'll get a sense of it early on, but you won't know how many times or items you need to do or find, respectively. The problem is that roughly halfway through the story, you pass a point of no return. You'll know it when you come to it. If you haven't achieved certain milestones before that point, the game is already over. You've already lost, but you may not know it for another hour or two.

To the authors' credit, the book does acknowledge this. It states plainly that you may need to play through more than once to complete it. So it wasn't a surprise that I needed two runs. And the second run was considerably easier, since I already knew what was behind many of the doors. Still, I lost my first run not through any mistake I made per se, but through a lack of knowledge of what winning even required. That does feel a little unfair.

The mood of the book is interesting. It's a little eerie, and not knowing what's behind each door makes opening them genuinely fun. However, it also feels a bit confused. The issue is that there's creatures of all kinds throughout, rather than the inhabitants of the mountain being a coherent grouping. I'm not advocating that all the creatures be of the same type; that would be awful. But if you can think of a mythical creature, then it probably features somewhere in The Warlock of Firetop Mountain. It has the effect of making the entire book feel lesser than the sum of its parts. Variety is great, but too much variety erodes at the immersion.

But there are plenty of positives. The writing is simple but fun, and Jackson and Livingstone make great use of the limited words available for each section. Smell, in particular, is invoked repeatedly throughout the book. Initially I found this odd, and it took me out of the experience briefly each time a new room was described in terms of its scent. But I soon realised how effective it was. Sight, sound, even touch simply don't evoke atmosphere the way smell does. Describing a room as being filled with cobwebs doesn't land the same way as describing it as having a musty smell. It's a small, shrewd choice, but it gives the dungeon a texture that a lesser book wouldn't bother with.

The story is rudimentary but perfectly passable. You're an adventurer seeking treasure from within the eponymous mountain. That's about it. What gives it life is the ambiguity of its encounters. Some are obviously hostile; most are not immediately so, and the moment of decision (do you attack, talk, run?) is often more engaging than the fight that may or may not follow. Combat is enjoyable and some encounters have interesting quirks, but it was the sections just before a fight that I enjoyed most.

Most of the encounters were fun, but one particular area did stand out as being... less so. There is a section which is literally called a maze, and if you don't track your movements, you can find yourself going over the same sections multiple times. This is a manageable annoyance, but certain actions in this area can also trigger multiple, nearly identical hostile encounters. Fights are fun, but they begin to lose their appeal once you've done them three or more times. I won't hold this against the book; it does tell you to track your movements, and I chose to ignore that the first time because I'm an idiot. Word of advice: don't be an idiot like me, and keep track of where you've been.

Perhaps I was just lucky with my rolls, but the fights didn't especially challenge me. I was never in serious danger during either run. With ten provisions restoring four stamina each, and skill and luck that held up throughout, the mountain felt more like an exploration than a survival challenge. Whether that's a feature or a flaw probably depends on what you're looking for.

Verdict

The Warlock of Firetop Mountain is a fun, sometimes frustrating experience, and a fantastic entry point into Fighting Fantasy and gamebooks more generally. Its flaws are real, chiefly that it withholds the rules of winning until it's too late to do anything about it, but it would be unfair to judge it too harshly. This was the very first Fighting Fantasy book and the format was still being invented. It's earned its place as one of the most important books in the genre, and it deserves to be read in that spirit.
