Fighting Fantasy Review

The Warlock of Firetop Mountain —
Where It All Began

Forty-odd years on, Steve Jackson and Ian Livingstone's original gamebook still holds a peculiar power over the imagination. I died seventeen times. I cheated twice. I have no regrets.

There is a moment in The Warlock of Firetop Mountain where you stand at a junction — left, right, or straight ahead — and you have absolutely no idea which way to go. No clue. No environmental hint. No helpful NPC muttering warnings. Just a paragraph number for each direction and your own nerve.

This is the book in miniature. Steve Jackson and Ian Livingstone published it in 1982 as a sort of experiment: could you take the adventure of a tabletop roleplaying game and compress it into something a single person could hold in their hands? The answer, it turned out, was yes — and the resulting book sold over two million copies and launched a series that ran to over sixty titles.

I came to it late. Very late. As a child I read a couple and loved them, but my mother considered them not proper reading and they quietly disappeared from my life. So my first real run-through of Firetop Mountain happened as an adult, sitting at a kitchen table with two dice and a pencil, feeling simultaneously ridiculous and completely absorbed.

What you're actually doing

You are an unnamed adventurer. You have heard rumours of a great treasure locked in the dungeon beneath Firetop Mountain. You have decided, for reasons the book does not dwell on, that you are going to go and get it. That is the entirety of the setup. The book is not interested in your backstory.

You have SKILL, STAMINA, and LUCK — each rolled with dice at the start, each determinative. A bad SKILL roll means you will struggle in every single combat. A bad STAMINA roll means you will die to things that shouldn't kill you. LUCK is used in specific situations and can be tested: test it often enough and it decreases, making future tests more likely to fail. The system is elegantly vicious.

The dungeon doesn't care about your feelings. It cares about your SKILL score, your remaining STAMINA points, and whether you happened to pick up a specific item three hours ago.

Combat works like this: you and a monster each have a SKILL score. Each round, you both roll two dice and add your SKILL. Higher total wins and deals damage to the other's STAMINA. Repeat until someone reaches zero. It's simple, it's tense, and it means a high-SKILL fighter genuinely has a different experience than a low-SKILL one.

Does it hold up?

Largely, yes — with caveats. The dungeon feels genuinely dungeon-like: a maze of rooms, each with something to interact with, some threatening and some offering cryptic assistance. There's an orc's kitchen. A ferryman on an underground river. A room full of sleeping barbarians you'd very much rather not wake. The world-building is thin by modern standards but it has texture.

What doesn't hold up as well is the key system. To win — genuinely win, not just kill the Warlock — you need two specific keys, collected from two specific rooms. If you missed either room, you can defeat the Warlock and still lose. There's no hint that you need them until you're standing at the locked chest. This is the kind of design that would be savaged in a modern review. In 1982, it was considered normal.

The instadeath paragraphs are also... numerous. Open the wrong door. Accept the wrong offer. Drink the wrong potion. The book contains a remarkable number of ways to simply die with no warning and no recourse. Whether this is a feature or a bug depends entirely on your tolerance for replaying sections from the beginning.

A word about cheating

I cheated twice. Once to see a path I'd missed, once because I'd invested two hours and wasn't prepared to die to a random LUCK test. I have no guilt about this. The book is forty-three years old and I'm reading it for the experience, not the achievement.

This is, I think, the correct attitude to bring to vintage gamebooks. They are not always fair. They were designed for a different era's expectations of what "fair" meant. Play with your finger in the page. Roll again if you roll a one. The point is the journey through the dungeon, not the validation of your LUCK stat.

Verdict

A flawed classic that earns its reputation. The dungeon is atmospheric, the system is elegant, and the urge to explore every branch is real. The key puzzle is maddening and the instadeaths are relentless, but this is where it all started — and you can feel the genuine excitement of people inventing something new. Essential reading for anyone serious about the genre.

Next up: The Citadel of Chaos, also by Steve Jackson, which I'm told is a significant improvement on the formula. I've already bought a copy. My pencil is sharp.
